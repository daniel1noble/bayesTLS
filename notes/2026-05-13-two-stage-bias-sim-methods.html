Methods — joint-4PL vs. classical two-stage bias simulation

Author

Daniel Noble (with Claude assistance)

Published

May 13, 2026

Draft methods section for a controlled simulation that compares the joint Bayesian 4PL estimator developed in this paper against the classical two-stage TDT pipeline that is the field’s current default. This note documents exactly what has been done — DGP, estimators, sample sizes, and the diagnostic checks — so the text can be moved into ms/supplement.qmd after review. It deliberately does not make claims about which estimator is preferable; the results and their interpretation are reported elsewhere.

1 Data-generating process

Each simulated dataset is one factorial experiment with five assay temperatures (\(T \in \{30, 32, 34, 36, 38\}\,^\circ\text{C}\)) and six exposure durations (\(t \in \{1, 5, 15, 45, 135, 405\}\) minutes). Each (temperature × duration) cell is replicated by \(n_\text{reps}\) independent cups. The per-cup sample size \(N\) is drawn from a discrete uniform on \(\{10, 11, \dots, 20\}\), capturing the modest, unbalanced replication typical of empirical TDT assays.

The latent survival probability follows a beta-binomial 4PL with temperature acting only on the midpoint:

\[ p(T, t) \;=\; \ell \;+\; \frac{u - \ell}{1 + \exp\!\bigl[k\bigl(\log_{10} t - m(T)\bigr)\bigr]}, \qquad m(T) = \beta_0 + \beta_1\,(T - \bar T). \]

Truth parameters were fixed at \(\ell = 0.05\), \(u = 0.92\), \(k = 8\), \(\beta_0 = 1.5\), \(\beta_1 = -0.15\), \(\bar T = 34\,^\circ\text{C}\), and beta-binomial dispersion \(\phi = 5\). The corresponding analytical targets, derived in closed form from the DGP, are

\[ z_\text{true} \;=\; -\frac{1}{\beta_1} \;=\; 6.667\,^\circ\text{C}, \qquad \text{CT}_{\max,\,1\text{h}}^{\text{true}} \;=\; \bar T \;+\; \frac{\log_{10} 60 - \beta_0 - k^{-1}\log\!\bigl[(u - 0.5)/(0.5 - \ell)\bigr]}{\beta_1} \;=\; 32.094\,^\circ\text{C}. \]

These are the per-simulation reference values against which both estimators are scored. Survival counts are drawn beta-binomially: for each cell, the cup-level probability \(p^{(b)}\) is drawn from a \(\text{Beta}(p\phi, (1-p)\phi)\) distribution and the observed survivor count from a \(\text{Binomial}(N, p^{(b)})\). The data generator is in scripts/sim_twostage_helpers.R.

2 Two design points

We ran the full simulation under two replication budgets, chosen to bracket realistic small-to-moderate field designs:

Design \(n_\text{reps}\) per cell Total cups per dataset
n3 3 90
n5 5 150

For each design we ran \(N_\text{sim} = 1000\) datasets. The number of simulations was set so that Monte Carlo standard errors (MCSEs) on the performance summaries fell below pre-specified thresholds (see Sample size adequacy).

3 Estimators

Both estimators were applied to every simulated dataset, scored against the same analytical truth, and summarised on the same basis (signed bias of the point estimate, root-mean-square error, empirical 95% interval coverage, and median 95% interval width).

3.1 Joint Bayesian 4PL

The joint model is the estimator developed in this paper. It is a non-linear brms formula with a beta_binomial(link = "identity") family in which all four 4PL sub-parameters — the reparameterised lower asymptote, upper asymptote, slope, and midpoint — carry a centred-temperature slope. Default priors from bayesTLS::make_4pl_priors() were used (weakly informative; specified in the supplement). Samples were drawn with cmdstanr (2 chains × 2,000 iterations, half discarded as warm-up; adapt_delta = 0.9, max_treedepth = 12). Post-fit, z and \(\text{CT}_{\max,\,1\text{h}}\) were extracted with bayesTLS::extract_tdt(..., lethal = TRUE) using the standard rate-multiplier definition of \(T_\text{crit}\); we report the posterior median and 2.5% / 97.5% quantiles.

3.2 Classical two-stage

The two-stage pipeline implements the field-standard recipe. Stage 1: for each temperature independently, a binomial GLM with logit link is fitted to the survivor counts as a function of \(\log_{10} t\); the per-temperature \(\log_{10} \text{LT}_{50}\) is read off as \(-\hat\beta_0/\hat\beta_1\) with standard error supplied by the delta method from the Stage-1 covariance. Stage 2: an unweighted ordinary least-squares regression of those \(\log_{10}\text{LT}_{50}\) values on temperature gives slope and intercept estimates from which \(z = -1/\hat\beta_1^{(2)}\) and \(\text{CT}_{\max,\,1\text{h}} = (\log_{10} 60 - \hat\beta_0^{(2)})/\hat\beta_1^{(2)}\) follow. The 95% confidence interval is obtained by the delta method applied to the Stage-2 covariance. A simulated dataset is marked as two-stage success only when every temperature delivered a finite, negative-slope Stage-1 fit and the Stage-2 slope itself was finite and negative.

Unweighted OLS at Stage 2 was retained deliberately, matching the version of the pipeline most commonly reported in the empirical TDT literature. SE-weighted alternatives were not assessed in this run.

4 Sample size adequacy

Each performance summary \(\widehat{Q}\) was reported with a Monte Carlo standard error: \(\text{MCSE}(\widehat{\text{bias}}) = s_b / \sqrt{N_\text{sim}}\) for mean bias, \(\sqrt{p(1-p)/N_\text{sim}}\) for coverage, and the standard delta-method form for RMSE. We pre-specified MCSE thresholds of 0.05 °C for bias and 0.015 for coverage. At \(N_\text{sim} = 1000\) both designs met the thresholds for every method × quantity combination, so we did not extend the simulation.

5 Diagnostic and reproducibility checks

Each joint-4PL fit logs maximum \(\hat R\) and divergent-transition count post-warm-up. Across all 2,000 fits (n3 + n5) no fit exceeded the standard \(\hat R < 1.05\) threshold, and ≥ 95 % of fits returned zero divergences.

The full set of per-simulation results, including all \(1{,}000\) posterior draws for \(z\), \(\text{CT}_{\max,\,1\text{h}}\) and \(T_\text{crit}\) per simulation, is stored as resume-safe per-sim RDS files under output/sim_twostage/raw/<design>/ and rolled up into summary_<design>.rds, meta_<design>.rds, and diffs_<design>.rds at the end of each run. The driver is scripts/sim_twostage_bias.R; the data-generating helpers and the two estimator wrappers live in scripts/sim_twostage_helpers.R. Parallelism uses a PSOCK cluster (parallel::makeCluster + parLapply) with five workers; a PSOCK cluster (rather than mclapply) is required because cmdstanr launches Stan executables via processx, which is not fork-safe under macOS.

6 Open scope

This first simulation block holds the data-generating process fixed in two respects: (i) temperature acts only on the 4PL midpoint, leaving the asymptotes (\(\ell\), \(u\)) and slope (\(k\)) constant in \(T\); and (ii) the joint-4PL estimator uses the package’s default priors, with no sensitivity analysis. Both restrictions are deliberate first-pass simplifications. Extensions that vary the DGP (temperature acting on \(\ell\), \(u\), or \(k\), with or without midpoint preservation) and that vary the joint-4PL priors are the natural next steps.
