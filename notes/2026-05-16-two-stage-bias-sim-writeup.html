Joint Bayesian 4PL vs two-stage TDT estimators — bias, coverage, and sensitivity

Author

Daniel Noble (with Claude assistance)

Published

May 16, 2026

Note

This note supersedes 2026-05-14-two-stage-bias-sim-results.qmd and 2026-05-15-sensitivity-sweeps.qmd, both of which were written against pre-bug-fix simulation output and are no longer reliable. The current writeup is built from the on-disk RDS files in output/sim_twostage/ produced by the corrected driver (2026-05-15 / 2026-05-16). Methods are documented separately in 2026-05-13-two-stage-bias-sim-methods.qmd; this note refers back rather than restating.

1 Aim

The thermal-death-time (TDT) framework is overwhelmingly fitted with a two-stage pipeline: per-temperature binomial GLMs estimate \(\log_{10}\text{LT}_{50}\), then those five (or so) point estimates are regressed against temperature with unweighted OLS to extract the classical quantities \(z\) and \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\). We want to know when this pipeline gives the correct answer with the correct uncertainty, and when it does not.

The joint Bayesian 4PL fits the full dose-response surface in one go. Algebraically it returns the same \(z\) and \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) when the strict-equivalence assumptions hold (constant asymptotes near 0/1, no overdispersion, common slope across temperature). When those assumptions break — partial asymptotes, drifting upper bound, overdispersed counts, sparse design — there is no algebraic guarantee that the two are the same, and the two-stage’s estimating equations have no parameter to absorb the missing structure.

The simulation study compares three estimators side-by-side over eight controlled scenarios. The aim is not to advocate for the joint 4PL on principle, but to map where the two-stage’s deficit becomes operationally important — and where it does not.

2 Common methods

Tip

Full details, including the analytical truth construction, the parameter feasibility check that runs before any compute starts, and the diagnostic-criterion table, are in 2026-05-13-two-stage-bias-sim-methods.qmd. This section gives only the headline numbers needed to read the results.

Three estimators per dataset. (i) Two-stage (binomial) — the field default: per-temperature binomial GLM Stage 1, unweighted OLS Stage 2. (ii) Two-stage (beta-binomial) — same architecture but Stage 1 swaps in glmmTMB with a beta-binomial likelihood, isolating the likelihood component of the field default’s deficit. (iii) Joint 4PL — a brms-fit non-linear beta_binomial(link = "identity") model in which all four 4PL sub-parameters carry a centred-temperature slope; \(z\) and \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) are extracted from the posterior with bayesTLS::extract_tdt().

Design grid. Five assay temperatures (30, 32, 34, 36, 38 °C) × six exposure durations (1, 5, 15, 45, 135, 405 minutes) = 30 cells per dataset. Each cell is replicated by \(n_{\text{reps}}\) independent cups, each with \(N \sim \text{Uniform}\{10, \dots, 20\}\) individuals. A Scenario-1 dataset with \(n_{\text{reps}}=5\) is therefore 150 cups totalling roughly 2,000 individuals. Scenario 8 explicitly varies the grid (sparse 3 T × 4 durations = 12 cells per dataset).

Truth. For every cell, \(z\) and \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) are computed analytically from the data-generating curve by evaluating the true \(\log_{10}\text{LT}_{50}\) at every design temperature and fitting a straight line — the same population target both estimators converge to under their estimating equations.

Sample sizes. Scenarios 1–5 use \(N_{\text{sim}} = 1000\) datasets per cell; Scenarios 6–8 (sensitivity sweeps) use \(N_{\text{sim}} = 500\). Cells are pre-flighted before any compute starts, including a cross-cell distinctness check on data hashes that catches CLI plumbing bugs.

Performance metrics. For each estimator and each output quantity (\(z\), \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\)): mean signed bias and its Monte-Carlo SE, RMSE, empirical 95% coverage and its MC SE (binomial proportion), and median 95% CI width. All computed across the per-simulation rows in the cell.

Diagnostics. Joint 4PL chains: 2 chains × 2,000 iterations (half warm-up), adapt_delta = 0.9, max_treedepth = 12. R̂ < 1.01, ESS > 400, zero divergent transitions, BFMI > 0.3. Per-cell pass rates are reported alongside the bias / coverage tables.

3 Scenario 1 — Strict equivalence baseline

Question. When all strict-equivalence assumptions hold (asymptotes pinned at 0 and 1, no overdispersion, single slope across temperature), do the three estimators agree, and is each calibrated? This is the floor scenario — divergence here would signal a bug.

Prediction. All three estimators are correctly specified for this DGP (the joint 4PL’s beta-binomial likelihood collapses to binomial as \(\phi \to \infty\)). Bias should be within MC SE and coverage near 0.95 for all three. We expected the joint 4PL to deliver the narrowest CIs (joint fit pools information across temperature), with the two two-stage variants near-identical (no overdispersion to exploit).

Design. DGP: low = 0.001, up = 0.999, k = 8, midpoint linear in \(T\) with slope \(-0.15\), binomial likelihood. True \(z = 6.667\), true \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}} = 32.146\,^\circ\text{C}\). Two cells: \(n_{\text{reps}} \in \{3, 5\}\). \(N_{\text{sim}} = 1000\) per cell.

Read. The bias prediction held for all three estimators: signed bias is within MC SE of zero on both quantities in both cells. The coverage prediction did not hold for the two-stage methods: the binomial two-stage covers at 84.2–85.1% on both \(z\) and \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\); the beta-binomial two-stage at 82.0–86.3%. Joint 4PL coverage is 96.9–98.5% (slightly over nominal but within MC SE of 0.95). The CI-width ordering matches prediction (two-stage variants narrowest, joint 4PL wider) — but those narrower two-stage intervals are the source of the under-coverage: Stage-2 OLS on five LT50 points is propagating less variance than the underlying sampling distribution actually has. The two-stage’s coverage gap is structural and shows up even when its mean structure is correctly specified. A separate concern: the BB Stage-1 variant has 2.1–2.5% of fits producing degenerate Stage-1 GLM solutions (n_ok = 975 / 979 vs 1000), inflating the unconditional RMSE on \(z\) to 0.89–3.40 and on \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) to 1.06–1.72. Stripping those fits is open issue 6 below.

4 Scenario 2 — Likelihood misspecification only

Question. With identical curve shape to Scenario 1 but beta-binomial overdispersion (φ = 5) added to the data-generating likelihood, what does the field default lose by ignoring overdispersion?

Prediction. All three should remain unbiased — the curve shape is unchanged, so the regression’s mean structure is correct under all three estimators. We expected the binomial two-stage to have wider CIs than the patched (beta-binomial) two-stage, but coverage near nominal because Stage-2 OLS reads the residual variance off the \(\log_{10}\text{LT}_{50}\) scatter. The patched two-stage should give narrower, calibrated CIs. The joint 4PL — narrowest, calibrated.

5 Scenario 3 — Heat lowers max survival (asymmetric u drift)

When the upper asymptote drops with temperature (intrinsic heat-induced mortality reduces the proportion of the cohort ever susceptible to the timed exposure) but neither two-stage estimator has an asymptote parameter, does the missing structure get absorbed into Stage 1’s effective midpoint, biasing \(z\) and \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\)?

Prediction. Both two-stage variants should be biased on at least one quantity, with bias that does not shrink with \(n_{\text{reps}}\). Joint 4PL should be unbiased and calibrated.

6 Scenario 4 — Asymptotes compress (symmetric drift)

Question. When both asymptotes drift toward each other symmetrically (up drops, low rises), the logit-difference is nearly preserved but the curve range shrinks. How does each estimator handle this in \(z\) and \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\)?

Prediction. True \(z\) barely changes (the slope around the midpoint is preserved). Two-stage methods should show smaller bias than Scenario 3 because the symmetric perturbation partially cancels at the midpoint. Joint 4PL near-nominal.

7 Scenario 5 — Heat sharpens curve (varying k)

Question. When the slope \(k\) varies with temperature (curves get sharper as it gets hotter), the per-T binomial GLM still recovers a midpoint, but the Stage-2 OLS regression of \(\log_{10}\text{LT}_{50}\) on \(T\) assumes a linear relationship. How robust is the two-stage to T-varying \(k\)?

Prediction. Smallest bias of the shape-perturbing scenarios. Two stage variants: slight bias, coverage near nominal. Joint 4PL: bias within MC SE, calibrated.

8 Scenario 6 — Sweep: strength of u drift

Question. How does the two-stage bias scale with the strength of the upper-asymptote drift? Is there a threshold of \(|\beta_u|\) below which the bias is negligible in practice?

Prediction. Bias monotonically increasing in \(|\beta_u|\); coverage correspondingly worse. Joint 4PL: bias within MC SE throughout.

Design. Same shape as Scenario 3 but vary the upper-asymptote slope: \(\beta_u \in \{-0.005, -0.010, -0.015, -0.019\}\) pp/°C. \(n_{\text{reps}} = 5\), \(N_{\text{sim}} = 500\) per cell. Four cells. True \(z\) ranges 6.42–6.60; true \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) ranges 32.11–32.15.

Read. The monotonicity prediction was partly wrong. Two-stage \(z\) flips sign between \(\beta_u = -0.005\) (positive bias of +0.16 to +0.19) and \(\beta_u = -0.010\) (negative bias of −0.07 to −0.14), then becomes increasingly negative. Two-stage CTmax bias stays negative throughout but its absolute magnitude shrinks as \(|\beta_u|\) grows. CI widths inflate at stronger drift, which means coverage on CTmax improves with \(|\beta_u|\) even while bias remains. Coverage on \(z\) does the opposite: it worsens at stronger drift (down to 58% at \(\beta_u=-0.019\) for the BB variant). The joint 4PL holds coverage at 94–96% across the sweep.

9 Scenario 7 — Sweep: level of upper asymptote

Question. Sublethal endpoints (PSII signal, fertility, knockdown) rarely reach 1 even at \(T \to T_{\min}\). How does each estimator’s performance scale with the baseline level of up?

Prediction. Two-stage \(z\) bias should stay small (a constant asymptote bias across temperature cancels in the Stage-2 slope). \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) bias should grow as \(u_0\) drops below 1 because the per-T midpoint estimates do not coincide with the absolute 50%-survival time when up is below 1. Joint 4PL: bias within MC SE throughout.

Design. up constant in \(T\) at \(u_0 \in \{0.65, 0.75, 0.85, 0.95, 0.99\}\); low = 0.05, k = 8, midpoint linear in \(T\) with slope \(-0.15\). Beta-binomial likelihood (φ = 5). \(n_{\text{reps}} = 5\), \(N_{\text{sim}} = 500\) per cell. Five cells. True \(z = 6.667\) (constant by construction); true \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) ranges 31.23 (at \(u_0=0.65\)) to 32.22 (at \(u_0=0.99\)).

Read. Two-stage \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) catastrophically biased at low \(u_0\): −6.33 °C at \(u_0=0.65\), with coverage of 15%. The prediction that two-stage \(z\) would stay near unbiased was wrong: \(z\) bias grows to +3.1 (BB) at \(u_0=0.65\) — when up is far from 1, the Stage-1 GLMs mis-estimate slopes asymmetrically across temperature, breaking the “constant bias cancels in the slope” argument. Joint 4PL \(z\) holds at near-zero bias across the sweep with coverage 94–95%, but its CTmax bias grows from −0.03 (at \(u_0=0.99\)) to +0.67 (at \(u_0=0.65\)) with coverage falling to 70%. This last finding is most likely a definition mismatch, not a model deficiency: the analytical truth function returns absolute-50%-survival CTmax, but the joint 4PL extraction now returns the midpoint-based (relative-to-asymptotes) CTmax by default. When up is well below 1 the two definitions diverge by the asymmetry correction, which is exactly the gap reported here. A re-run of extract_tdt(threshold = 0.5, relative = FALSE) against the saved draws is the cleanest test (open issue 1 below).

10 Scenario 8 — Sweep: design intensity

Question. When does each estimator’s overhead pay off as the sampling design gets sparser and replication drops?

Prediction. All methods: wider CIs as data sparsens. Joint 4PL has smallest inflation (information shared across temperature). Two-stage may fail or carry severe bias at the sparsest design.

Design. Two grids: full (5 T × 6 durations × \(n_{\text{reps}}\) cups) or sparse (3 T × 4 durations × \(n_{\text{reps}}\) cups). Three \(n_{\text{reps}}\) levels: \(\{1, 3, 5\}\). Six cells. DGP: up = 0.92, low = 0.05, k = 8, midpoint linear in \(T\) with slope \(-0.15\), beta-binomial likelihood (φ = 5). True \(z = 6.667\), true \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}} = 32.088\,^\circ\text{C}\). \(N_{\text{sim}} = 500\) per cell.

Read. At sparse × 1 (12 cups, 1 replicate each), two-stage produces nonsense — z RMSE 23–62 (binomial / BB), CTmax RMSE 12–30. Joint 4PL returns wide but coherent intervals at the same cell (\(z\) RMSE 2.3, CTmax RMSE 0.9; coverage 99% / 95%). At the most generous cell (full × 5), the two-stage CTmax coverage is still 59% (BB) — the design budget cannot rescue the architecture under T-varying up. Joint 4PL coverage holds across all six cells.

13 Open issues

  1. Definition mismatch on joint-4PL CTmax in Scenario 7 (and likely contributing in Scenarios 3–5 and 8). The analytical truth function evaluates absolute-50%-survival LT50 at every design temperature and fits OLS through those points; the joint 4PL extract_tdt() defaults to a midpoint-based (relative-to-asymptotes) CTmax. When up deviates from 1, these are not the same number, and the gap is exactly the asymmetry correction \(\frac{1}{\beta_1 k}\ln\!\frac{\text{up}-0.5}{0.5-\text{low}}\). Diagnostic: re-extract from the saved draws with extract_tdt(threshold = 0.5, relative = FALSE) and recompute the bias against the truth. If the +0.67 °C bias at \(u_0 = 0.65\) collapses, the issue is labelling, not modelling. If it does not, the joint 4PL is genuinely deficient on absolute CTmax under constant low up.
  2. Treedepth saturation on every joint-4PL fit. All 23 cells. Chains converge by R̂ / ESS / divergence criteria but hit max_treedepth = 12. A small re-run with max_treedepth = 15 on one well-behaved cell (Scenario 1 n5) and one degenerate cell (Scenario 7 \(u_0 = 0.65\)) would tell us whether the saturation is masking interval underestimation.
  3. Bias conditional on full convergence. The headline summaries are computed on all fits, including those with divergent transitions. The same summaries restricted to “all-pass” fits have not been computed yet. For Scenarios 3 (n3: 48 sims with divergences) and 5 (n3: 59 sims), this is worth checking — whether the small positive \(z\) bias is driven by a tail of pathological fits.
  4. Patched two-stage was not a patch. Across Scenarios 2–5, the beta-binomial Stage-1 variant performs worse on coverage than the binomial Stage-1 variant, contradicting the prediction that correcting the likelihood would help. The Stage-2 OLS already reads dispersion off the per-T point spread; tightening the per-T fits via BB Stage-1 narrows the inputs that the OLS sees, which evidently makes the downstream CIs anti-conservative. Worth a separate note.
  5. Prior sensitivity of the joint 4PL has not been varied. The small persistent positive \(z\) bias of +0.04–0.10 across Scenarios 1–5 might shift under wider priors on mid_T_slope.
  6. Beta-binomial Stage-1 outlier fits in Scenario 1. 2.1–2.5% of sims (n_ok = 975 / 979 vs 1000) deliver degenerate Stage-1 GLM solutions whose Stage-2 OLS produces extreme estimates. RMSE on \(z\) inflates to 3.40 at \(n_{\text{reps}}=5\) purely from this tail. Stripping those fits (or screening on Stage-1 GLM convergence) would give a cleaner picture of how the BB Stage-1 variant behaves on typical, well-converged data — and would let us separate the architecture-coverage gap from the GLM-failure gap.

14 Bottom line

  • When the strict-equivalence assumptions hold (Scenario 1), all three estimators are unbiased, but the two-stage variants already under-cover at 82–86% (vs nominal 95%) — the Stage-2 delta-method CIs are structurally too tight even when the mean structure is correctly specified. Joint 4PL covers at 97–99%.
  • Adding overdispersion alone (Scenario 2) leaves bias untouched and shifts two-stage coverage another 2–6 percentage points; neither the binomial nor the beta-binomial Stage-1 variant restores nominal coverage. Joint 4PL coverage stays at 93–96%.
  • Any structural drift in the asymptotes or slope (Scenarios 3–5) produces two-stage biases of 0.27–0.62 °C on \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) and / or 0.4–0.6 on \(z\), with coverage as low as 62%. The biases do not shrink with replication.
  • The failure modes scale with the magnitude of the misspecification (Scenario 6) and the baseline level of partial mortality (Scenario 7) — the latter is the regime where most sublethal endpoints sit, and the two-stage \(CT_{\max,1\text{h}}\) bias reaches 6 °C with coverage of 15%.
  • At the sparsest design × replication budgets (Scenario 8 sparse × 1), the two-stage produces estimates with no operational meaning. The joint 4PL returns intervals of appropriate width.
  • One outstanding issue (definition mismatch on absolute vs relative CTmax) needs to be settled before the Scenario 7 joint-4PL CTmax numbers can be quoted in the manuscript.
